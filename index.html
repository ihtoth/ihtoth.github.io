2

Graphic Design. I don’t want to create anything that wastes people’s time. As a designer, it is my responsibility to avoid design patterns that cause anxiety and confusion.

My first job out in the wild was an Automation Software Engineer at Intel. As a newly minted adult, I wanted most of all to please my coworkers and managers. I did exactly what I was told: learn fast, and program things due yesterday. In the process, I ignored certain thoughts which got louder and louder as time passed: Why do the users turn off our automation and do things manually essentially abandoning the software we wrote? Why am I not allowed to talk to them and watch them work on new releases to work out any kinks?

When I voiced my concern to management, they loudly ignored what I said, citing my proposed improvements a “waste of time” and “no competition, internal customers…it doesn’t matter...” and then put me on a project the furthest away from UX they could find: how to take the user out of the picture entirely. Their vision was a lights-off factory.

Needless to say, this attitude and short sightedness made my blood boil. Yes I get all the reasons, but it didn’t mean I was ok with it. I finally realized that this was not the place for me, because the type of work I enjoyed the most: talking to customers about frustrations and ultimately design for them, was at risk of falling off the priority list entirely.

2

UX Ethics. I have a degree in this too. Back in the day, apps and interface design were in their infancy, left mainly for the programmer to implement. I sort of though it was dumb to design print brochures, so I got a job as a programmer...

Not all apps are created equal: some are confusing, others draw you into an endless scrolling abyss, some make you feel overwhelmed by revealing too much visually. My favorite apps are the ones I spend the least time with: they tell me what I want to know, help me with making my micro-decision, and lets me move on with life. It’s like my favorite shoe that I think about when putting on in the morning, but actively forget about while “carrying” it around with me all day.

I want to create design that doesn’t include nefarious dark patterns, pulling people in like a casino does with a mixture of anxiety and dopamine. I’d rather create warm-fuzzy-clarity type design that brings people back because it truly helps them reach their goals without distraction. I believe reducing anxiety and depression is my purpose as a designer. This doesn’t mean getting rid of technology, but being smarter in design to reduce unnecessary screen time thereby allowing for more community and human connection.
